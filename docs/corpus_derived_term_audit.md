# Corpus-Derived Term Audit

Last updated: 2026-06-04

## Purpose

This document records a first audit of apparently incoherent corpus-derived terms by cohort. The goal is to identify lexical artifacts, boilerplate contamination, and overly broad screening rules before using the inductive term inventory for phenomenological or forensic/legal interpretation.

The term inventory is generated by `scripts/extract_corpus_derived_terms.py` from the deduplicated report-level corpus and the current screening outputs. It should be treated as a calibration and hypothesis-generation layer, not as validated coding.

## Why `all_reports` Exists

`all_reports` is not an analytic cohort in the same sense as `fear_of_death`, `arrest_detention_custody`, or `lsd_lysergamides`.

It is retained as a background vocabulary for three reasons:

1. It shows which words are frequent across the whole corpus and therefore not specific to any clinical or forensic cohort.
2. It provides the background denominator used to calculate cohort specificity, including log-odds and background document percentage.
3. It helps detect boilerplate and extraction artifacts. Terms that are frequent in `all_reports` but not clinically meaningful are candidates for the boilerplate or stopword list.

Interpretation rule: `all_reports` should be used for quality control and statistical background only. It should not be read as a phenomenological category.

## Corrections Applied

The following corrections were applied after inspecting the initial inventory:

| Area | Problem Found | Correction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Erowid editorial notes | Editorial warnings such as driving while intoxicated created false `traffic_driving_accident` and public-safety terms. | Bracketed `[Erowid note: ...]` text is now removed before serious-event detection and corpus-term extraction. |
| Arrest/detention | `cell`, `cell phone`, and `cardiac arrest` contaminated the arrest/custody cohort. | Arrest terms were restricted to legal forms such as `arrested`, `under arrest`, `arrest warrant`, and `resisting arrest`; `cell` now requires a jail/prison/holding/police context. |
| Assault/violence | `kicked` captured idioms such as "kicked in" effects; `stabbing` captured somatic pain. | Kicking and stabbing patterns now require clearer interpersonal violence wording. |
| Defenestration/fall | Greedy patterns such as `jumped .* balcony` captured reports merely mentioning a balcony later in the sentence. | Jump/fall patterns now require directional prepositions such as `from`, `off`, `out of`, or `over`. |
| Death/fatality | Generic `fatal` was too broad for actual death/fatality reports. | Fatality patterns now require more specific contexts such as fatal overdose, reaction, accident, dose, intoxication, or poisoning. |
| Loss of control | `spiral` captured Erowid merchandise boilerplate and other non-phenomenological text. | `spiral` now requires contexts such as `spiraling out`, `downward spiral`, or `thought spiral`. |
| Fear of death | Broad `death` and `dead` captured music references, ego-death terms, and boilerplate. | Fear-of-death patterns now require phrases indicating dying, feared death, near-death, overdose, heart attack, or first-person deadness. |
| Corpus boilerplate | Terms related to Erowid ads and merchandise appeared in top n-grams. | Additional boilerplate terms were added to the corpus-derived term extractor. |

## Current Cohort-Level Audit

### `arrest_detention_custody`

Current top terms are coherent overall: `jail`, `arrested`, `prison`, `handcuffs`, `booked`, `dui`, `felony`, `probation`, `bail`, `charges`, `lawyer`, `police station`.

Residual caution: `undercover`, `defend`, and some county/officer terms may reflect narrative context rather than a confirmed legal outcome. These should remain in the human-review queue.

### `assault_violence_weapon`

The most visible false positives from the initial pass were reduced. Current coherent terms include `violent`, `knife`, `gun`, `punched`, `stabbed`, `assault`, `weapon`, `strangled`, `restraints`, `attacked`, `injuries`, `armed`.

Residual caution: terms such as `reveals`, `filming`, `defend`, and `argue` are contextual and should not be interpreted as violence without report-level review.

### `traffic_driving_accident`

The Erowid editorial-note artifact was removed. Current terms include plausible signal terms: `car accident`, `dui`, `hit car`, `accident`, `traffic`, `crash`, `driver`, `drove`, `driving`, `parking lot`, `road`.

Residual caution: generic terms such as `car`, `windows`, `station`, `dealer`, or `money` are not independently meaningful. This cohort should be interpreted at the event level, not from isolated top words.

### `composite_public_safety_endangerment`

This composite remains heterogeneous by design. It now contains terms from traffic accidents, endangerment, charges, and police station contexts.

Residual caution: because this is a composite endpoint, top terms mix several mechanisms. It is useful for screening public-safety relevance, but paper figures should usually decompose it into traffic, assault/violence, endangerment of others, and custody/legal subdomains.

### `death_fatality_reported`

Current terms include `fatal`, `died`, `overdose`, `dying`, `seizure`, `die`, `death`, `lived`.

Residual caution: this cohort still requires careful manual review because death-related vocabulary can refer to fear of dying, ego death, overdose risk, third-party deaths, or actual fatal outcomes. It should not be reported as fatality prevalence without validation.

### `fear_of_death`

The most obvious artifacts were reduced. Current terms are more aligned with the construct: `overdose`, `heart attack`, `near death`, `scared death`, `afraid die`, `fear dying`, `convinced die`, `barely breathing`.

Residual caution: `dead hell`, `words remember`, and other generic n-grams are not interpretable alone. This is a strong candidate for a supervised validation set, because the difference between "I thought I was dying" and metaphoric death language is central to the phenomenological hypothesis.

### `loss_of_control`

Current terms are coherent: `stuck`, `trapped`, `loop`, `couldn't stop`, `stuck loop`, `trapped inside`, `stuck forever`, `downward spiral`, `feedback loop`, `couldn't control`, `lost control`.

Residual caution: terms such as `stuck finger` may indicate bodily or situational context rather than cognitive loss of control. These should be handled through report-level validation.

### `defenestration_jump_fall_height`

The broad balcony/rooftop artifact was reduced, but this remains a small and high-stakes cohort. Top terms include `jump window`, `jump`, `window`, `jumped`, `ambulance`, `screaming`, `running`, `ground`.

Residual caution: because this event type is rare and severe, every positive report should be manually reviewed before analysis or publication.

### `endangerment_of_others`

The vomiting-related `threw up` artifact was reduced. Current terms are still mixed and include `pushed`, `killed`, `pole`, `rear`, `pushing`, and several narrative-context terms.

Residual caution: this category is semantically difficult. It should be validated with a stricter human code distinguishing actual third-party harm, risk only, hypothetical harm, and unclear cases.

### `sexual_assault_exploitation`

Top terms include clear signal terms such as `raped`, `rape`, `sexual`, `naked`, but also contextual terms such as `pathetic`, `torture`, `people knew`, `pressed`, `bent`, `character`, `appearance`.

Residual caution: this should be treated as an ultra-sensitive screen-positive queue. No prevalence or interpretive claims should be made without manual validation and careful ethical wording.

### Substance Cohorts

`mdma_entactogens` still shows ketamine terms, and `other_tryptamines` shows methamphetamine/methoxetamine terms.

Interpretation: this is not necessarily a dictionary error. The substance groups are currently report-level and multi-label: one report can involve MDMA and ketamine, or a tryptamine with stimulants or dissociatives. For molecule-level comparisons, the analysis should distinguish:

1. primary substance reports;
2. any-exposure reports;
3. polysubstance reports;
4. reports with interpretable dose units.

## Recommended Next Use

Use the term inventory in three layers:

1. Quality control: identify artifacts like editorial notes, merchandise text, idioms, and broad regex mistakes.
2. Dictionary refinement: promote coherent recurrent terms to candidate rules only after report-level inspection.
3. Human validation: use the workbook queues to estimate precision, false-positive themes, and category definitions before making prevalence or comparative claims.

For publication, report that the keyword and n-gram inventory was used iteratively to audit and refine screen-positive cohorts, while final claims about severe adverse events and medico-legal outcomes depend on human validation.
